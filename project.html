Aim

Objective of the experiment

Understanding the Core Idea: Variable Treated as a Constant

This virtual lab experiment focuses on the concept of partial differentiation and the crucial technique of treating one variable as constant while differentiating with respect to another.

By the end of this experiment, students will be able to:

  • Understand the definition of partial differentiation and why other variables are treated as constants.
  • Learn step-by-step how to compute partial derivatives for functions of multiple variables.
  • Solve problems involving functions like f(x, y) = x²y + 3xy² by correctly isolating variation.
  • Apply the concept to real-world engineering problems in heat transfer, fluid mechanics, and optimization.

Theory: Variable Treated as Constant

Partial differentiation & multivariable calculus

A. Definition: Partial Differentiation

Partial differentiation is the process of differentiating a function of several variables with respect to one variable while keeping the other variables fixed. In other words, all other independent variables are treated as constants. This is the fundamental principle behind analyzing how a multivariable system changes when only one input varies.

B. Mathematical Explanation & Example

Consider a function of two variables: z = x²y + 3xy². To find the partial derivative with respect to x (∂z/∂x), we treat y as a constant:

∂z/∂x = 2x·y + 3·(1)·y² = 2xy + 3y²

For ∂z/∂y, treat x as constant:

∂z/∂y = x²·(1) + 3x·(2y) = x² + 6xy

C. Step-by-Step Method

  • Step 1: Identify the variable with respect to which you differentiate.
  • Step 2: Treat all other variables as constants (like numbers).
  • Step 3: Apply ordinary differentiation rules (power rule, product rule, chain rule).

D. Key Rules (Same as Ordinary Derivatives)

  • Power Rule: ∂(xⁿ)/∂x = n·xⁿ⁻¹ (others constant).
  • Constant Rule: Derivative of a constant term (w.r.t variable) = 0.
  • Product Rule: ∂(u·v)/∂x = u·(∂v/∂x) + v·(∂u/∂x).

E. Engineering Applications

Partial differentiation is essential in heat transfer (temperature gradients), fluid mechanics (velocity fields), economics (marginal analysis), and structural optimization. The concept of "treating variables as constants" lets engineers isolate effects.
